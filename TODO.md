## Methodology: known concerns and proposed solutions

The project's core question is "what (recall, precision) must a stock-picking
classifier achieve to beat the market, and by how much / how reliably?" The
items below are the open methodological gaps between the current simulation and
a defensible answer, grouped by the three original concerns plus newly
identified ones.

### 1. Choosing realistic selection criteria

The label target is a free parameter (`label_threshold`), but nothing yet tells
us which criteria are (a) learnable by a real model and (b) sufficient to beat
the market even at high precision.

- [ ] **Criterion feasibility screen (perfect-precision upper bound).** For each candidate criterion (e.g. `>0%`, `>10%`, `>benchmark`, `>benchmark+10%`), compute the return of the *precision = 1.0* portfolio per year (the equal-weighted mean of all stocks meeting the criterion). This is cheap — one pass over `stock_yearly`, no selection simulation — and answers the "shouldn't be taken for granted" question directly: if even a perfect classifier for ">0% over the year" doesn't beat SPY, no precision level rescues that criterion. Output a table: criterion × {per-year positive-set mean return, benchmark return, excess}.
- [ ] **Report base rate and lift alongside precision.** Precision is not comparable across criteria or years: 70% precision when 55% of stocks beat the threshold (lift ≈ 1.3) is a modest model; 70% precision at a 10% base rate (lift = 7) is an extraordinary one. Add per-year prevalence `T/(T+F)` to `custom_stats` and the sweep output, and report lift (`precision / base_rate`) in summaries and the heatmap so "realistic" can actually be judged. A model claim should be considered realistic in lift terms, not raw precision terms.
- [ ] **Derive (recall, precision) pairs from an assumed model skill level instead of free parameters.** A real classifier has one score and an ROC curve; recall and precision are not independently choosable. Model the classifier's score as a latent variable with a chosen AUC (binormal model is fine), then trace the implied precision-recall curve given each year's base rate, and sweep over *AUC* (e.g. 0.55–0.65, the range cross-sectional return-prediction literature suggests is achievable) and operating threshold. This turns "is (r=0.2, p=0.7) realistic?" into "what AUC does that require at this base rate?", which is answerable against published results.
- [ ] **Multi-year horizon support.** Criteria like ">0% annual returns over the next 3 years" require forward h-year returns; the pipeline currently only computes calendar-year returns. Parameterize a holding horizon: compute h-year forward returns from each formation year, decide overlapping (rolling yearly cohorts, more samples but autocorrelated) vs non-overlapping windows, and define what the portfolio does between rebalances. Note the universe/label bookkeeping: membership is taken at formation time; delistings inside the horizon use the last-available-price rule as today.

### 2. Portfolio weighting

- [ ] **Equal-weight benchmark comparison (quick win, do first).** The simulated portfolios are equal-weighted but the benchmark (SPY) is cap-weighted, so part of any measured "outperformance" is just the equal-weight/size effect, not model skill. Add an equal-weighted universe benchmark — `stock_yearly.mean(axis=1)` per year (or RSP for a tradable proxy, but the internal mean is bias-consistent with the portfolios) — and report excess vs both benchmarks everywhere (study summary, sweep, heatmap). This disentangles "model skill" from "weighting scheme" at near-zero cost.
- [ ] **Cap-weighted portfolio variant.** Requires point-in-time market caps (shares outstanding × price), which yfinance doesn't provide historically. Blocked on the Sharadar subscription (SF1/SEP include shares outstanding and marketcap); implement as an alternative aggregation in `model_selected_portfolio_return` (weights argument) so top-N, bottom-N, and custom portfolios all support it.
- [ ] **Confidence-weighted portfolios (the "most sensible" scheme, currently unsimulated).** Give the hypothetical model a *score*, not just a label decision: sample a synthetic score per stock from a latent-variable model calibrated to the target AUC / (recall, precision) — this is the same machinery as the ROC-based sweep above — then weight positions by calibrated probability (or Kelly fraction). Compare equal / cap / confidence weighting at the same confusion matrix to isolate the value of confidence information. Note this subsumes recall: a confidence-weighted portfolio over all names with score above a threshold *is* the natural output of a real model.

### 3. Risk / variance measurement

- [ ] **Path-level Monte Carlo — replace per-year q05 compounding.** `compute_cagr(custom_stats["q05"])` compounds the 5th-percentile return of *every* year, i.e. assumes the portfolio hits a 1-in-20 bad draw 13 years running (probability ~0.05¹³) — it's not a meaningful "worst case", it's an incoherent one, and it's currently the sweep's headline number. Instead simulate full paths: for each of K simulations, draw one portfolio per year, compound the drawn returns across the whole period, and take quantiles of the resulting *CAGR distribution*. Report `P(path CAGR < benchmark CAGR)` — the probability of underperforming over the full period — as the primary answer to "how often / what's the risk". Per-year q05/q95 stay useful as within-year dispersion diagnostics.
- [ ] **Analytic mean/variance of the selected portfolio.** No Monte Carlo needed for the first two moments: with TP positives and FP negatives drawn uniformly without replacement, the equal-weighted portfolio's expected return is exactly `precision · mean(positive returns) + (1 − precision) · mean(negative returns)` — notably *independent of recall* — and its variance is a hypergeometric-sampling expression in the within-class return variances and portfolio size (recall matters only through TP = round(recall·T), i.e. diversification). Implement these closed forms, verify against the simulator, and use them to make the sweep near-instant and exact. This largely completes the "expected returns and their variance" item above.
- [ ] **Empty/infeasible years must not silently drop out of the CAGR.** When a year has no feasible selection (e.g. `label_threshold=0.1` in a bear year with almost no positives), `custom_stats` gets NaN and `compute_cagr` *skips the year entirely*, as if the investor didn't exist that year — this biases exactly the years that matter most. Define an explicit fallback policy for years where the model makes no (or too few) picks — hold cash (0% or T-bill return) or hold the benchmark — thread it through `simulate_custom_portfolio_distribution`/`compute_cagr`, and report how many fallback years each configuration hit. Same policy applies to path simulation.
- [ ] **Richer risk metrics on simulated paths**: max drawdown of the growth path, tracking error vs benchmark, Sortino ratio, and worst single-year return — quantiles of CAGR alone hide sequencing risk.
- [ ] **Minimum-portfolio-size guard.** Low recall × few positives can yield portfolios of 1–5 names, where the q05 mostly measures idiosyncratic lottery, not strategy risk, and no real investor would run it. Report portfolio size per year; flag (or apply the fallback policy to) years below a size floor.

### 4. Additional concerns (newly identified)

- [ ] **Uniform-random errors misstate a real model's error structure.** `simulate_selection` draws false positives uniformly from all negatives, but a real model's FPs cluster near the decision boundary (stocks that *barely* missed the criterion — mild underperformers, not random blowups), which makes the uniform draw *pessimistic* on FP damage; meanwhile a real model's picks are correlated (sector/factor concentration), which makes the iid draw *optimistic* on variance. Add sampling modes to bound both effects: (a) *boundary-weighted* FPs — sample negatives with weight decreasing in distance below the threshold; (b) *adversarial* FPs — sample from the worst decile of negatives, as a stress floor; (c) *clustered* draws — sample TPs/FPs within randomly chosen sectors (needs a sector mapping, available from Sharadar or a static GICS file) to model correlated errors. Report the spread across modes.
- [ ] **Constant (recall, precision) across years is unrealistic.** Real model skill is regime-dependent, and precision most plausibly degrades exactly in down years — when false positives hurt most. Add (a) stochastic per-year precision, e.g. `precision_y ~ Beta` centered on the target, and (b) an adversarial regime stress: precision drops by a configurable amount in years where the benchmark return is negative. If conclusions survive (b), they're much stronger.
- [ ] **Year-to-year error independence is assumed.** Consecutive-year draws are independent, but a wrong model tends to stay wrong (its thesis persists). In path simulation, add optional persistence — e.g. correlate the per-year precision draws, or with clustered sampling re-use the same sector tilt across adjacent years.
- [ ] **No transaction costs, taxes, or expense-ratio asymmetry.** The strategy implies ~100% annual turnover of up to hundreds of equal-weighted positions, compared against SPY *net* of its expense ratio. Add a simple cost model (configurable bps × turnover, default ~10–20 bps round-trip) and optionally a tax drag toggle (annual realization vs SPY's deferral) to the yearly returns before compounding. Even crude costs will move the required-precision frontier.
- [ ] **Calendar-year cohorts are an arbitrary discretization.** Results may be sensitive to the January formation date (turn-of-year effects, one specific 12-month window per year). Sensitivity check: run overlapping 12-month cohorts starting each month (or quarter) and confirm the required-precision conclusions are stable across formation months. Fits naturally with the multi-year-horizon refactor.
- [ ] **Small and regime-biased year sample.** Default 2012–2024 is 13 observations from a mostly relentless bull market; "beats the market" conclusions may not transfer. Mitigations: extend back to 2000/1996 once the delisted-data provider lands (the current data can't support it honestly); bootstrap over years (resample years with replacement in path simulation) to widen the CAGR confidence bands; and report results split by regime (benchmark up-years vs down-years) so readers see where the edge comes from.
- [ ] **Feasibility tolerance and small-T rounding artifacts.** `simulate_custom_portfolio_distribution` declares a cell feasible only if achieved metrics land within ±0.1 of the request, and `TP = round(recall·T)` quantizes hard when T is small — so heatmap "n/a" cells can be rounding artifacts rather than truly infeasible requests, and achieved metrics can sit far from grid labels. Surface achieved-vs-requested deviation in the heatmap (annotate cells), make the tolerance a parameter, and consider targeting the closest *achievable* confusion matrix instead of rejecting.
