## Methodology: known concerns and proposed solutions

The project's core question is "what (recall, precision) must a stock-picking
classifier achieve to beat the market, and by how much / how reliably?" The
items below are the open methodological gaps between the current simulation and
a defensible answer, grouped by the three original concerns plus newly
identified ones.

### 1. Choosing realistic selection criteria

The label target is a free parameter (`label_threshold`), but nothing yet tells
us which criteria are (a) learnable by a real model and (b) sufficient to beat
the market even at high precision.

- [x] **Criterion feasibility screen (perfect-precision necessary condition).** For each candidate criterion (e.g. `>0%`, `>10%`, `>benchmark`, `>benchmark+10%`), compute the return of the *precision = 1.0, recall = 1.0* portfolio per year (the equal-weighted mean of all stocks meeting the criterion). This is cheap — one pass over `stock_yearly`, no selection simulation — and answers the "shouldn't be taken for granted" question directly: if even a perfect classifier for ">0% over the year" doesn't beat SPY, no precision level rescues that criterion. This is a *necessary-condition screen only*: a low-recall draw centers on the same mean but with real variance (and, since positive returns are right-skewed, a below-mean typical draw), so passing the screen is where the risk analysis below starts, not where it ends. Done: `screen_label_criteria`/`summarize_screen` (one pass over `stock_yearly`), exposed as the `screen` CLI subcommand — criteria specs are absolute (`0`, `0.1`) or benchmark-relative (`bmk`, `bmk+0.1`); prints the per-year criterion × {threshold, num_positive, base_rate, positive-set mean return, benchmark return, excess} table plus a per-criterion summary (`cagr_perfect` vs `cagr_benchmark`, `passes_screen` verdict) and writes `Criterion_Feasibility.csv`. Covered by `tests/test_screen.py`. *Note: `cagr_perfect` drops years with an empty positive set (reported as `years_no_positive`) — inherits the no-pick/cash-policy gap tracked under Risk measurement.*
- [x] **Report the per-year base rate (label prevalence) alongside results.** `T/(T+F)` is the precision a dart-throwing picker gets for free, so the sweep's "precision p suffices" is only interpretable as *p minus base rate* of skill on top of chance — in a year where 55% of stocks beat the threshold, precision 0.6 is nearly darts; at a 10% base rate it's a strong model. This also matters for proving the project thesis: "prefer precision over recall" is only demonstrated against criteria where the required precision is well above the free baseline. Note too that with a fixed absolute `label_threshold`, the base rate collapses in bear years, so holding precision constant quietly assumes the most skill exactly there — the per-year prevalence column makes that visible without assuming anything about how a real model behaves. Done: `simulate_custom_portfolio_distribution` now returns `base_rate`, threaded into `custom_stats` (per-year column), the study's `yearly` table (`label_base_rate`) and summary (`label_base_rate_mean/min/max`, `custom_precision_edge_mean`), and every sweep row (`base_rate_mean/min/max`, `precision_edge_mean` = achieved precision − base rate); the screen table reports per-criterion prevalence too. Covered by `test_base_rate_reporting` in `tests/test_sweep.py` and `tests/smoke_test.py`.
- [x] **Multi-year horizon support.** Criteria like ">0% annual returns over the next 3 years" require forward h-year returns; the pipeline currently only computes calendar-year returns. Parameterize a holding horizon: compute h-year forward returns from each formation year, decide overlapping (rolling yearly cohorts, more samples but autocorrelated) vs non-overlapping windows, and define what the portfolio does between rebalances. Note the universe/label bookkeeping: membership is taken at formation time; delistings inside the horizon use the last-available-price rule as today. Done: `--horizon H` and `--overlapping` on all three subcommands (`horizon`/`overlapping` parameters threaded from `prepare_universe_returns` through study/sweep/screen). Each cohort forms at the start of a formation year (membership snapshot at formation, as before) and holds buy-and-hold through year formation+H−1 (`year_universe_returns` gained a `horizon` parameter; delistings inside the window keep the last-available-price rule, counted under the pre-existing `partial_year` key). Both window schemes are supported rather than chosen: non-overlapping is the default (formation years step by H via `formation_years` — chaining those windows is a realizable rebalance-every-H-years path), `--overlapping` forms a cohort every year (more samples, autocorrelated; the study skips the growth plot there since compounding overlapping windows isn't a realizable path). Labeling thresholds and screen criteria stay *per-annum* rates, compounded to window totals internally (`compound_annual_threshold`; `bmk±offset` applies the offset to the benchmark's annualized window return), and every CAGR is annualized via `compute_cagr(..., years_per_period=H)` — for overlapping windows that equals the geometric mean of annualized cohort returns, a summary statistic rather than a path. Covered by `tests/test_horizon.py`; `horizon=1` reduces exactly to the old calendar-year behavior.

### 2. Portfolio weighting

- [x] **Equal-weight benchmark comparison (quick win, do first).** The simulated portfolios are equal-weighted but the benchmark (SPY) is cap-weighted, so part of any measured "outperformance" is just the equal-weight/size effect, not model skill. Done: `equal_weight_benchmark_returns` (`stock_yearly.mean(axis=1)`, bias-consistent with the portfolios) feeds an `ew_benchmark_return` column plus per-N `top{n}_excess_ew` into the study's yearly table, `avg_ew_benchmark_return`/`avg_excess_ew`/`cagr_ew_benchmark` into the summary, `cagr_ew_benchmark`/`custom_q05_meets_ew_benchmark` into every sweep row, a dashed equal-weight line into both study plots, and a second heatmap (`recall_precision_heatmap_ew.png`, via `plot_sweep_heatmap(benchmark_col=...)`). Covered by `tests/test_benchmarks_and_weights.py` and the extended `tests/test_sweep.py`/`tests/smoke_test.py`. *Labeling with `label_threshold=None` still targets the cap-weighted benchmark's return — only the performance comparison gained the second baseline. The `screen` subcommand reports both too (`ew_benchmark_return`/`excess_ew` per year; `avg_excess_ew`, `years_beating_ew_benchmark`, `cagr_ew_benchmark`, `passes_screen_ew` in the summary), with `bmk`-relative criteria still thresholded against the cap-weighted benchmark.*
- [ ] **Cap-weighted portfolio variant.** Requires point-in-time market caps (shares outstanding × price). No longer blocked: prices now come from Sharadar, whose DAILY/METRICS tables carry marketcap for the same tickers. *Aggregation side done: `portfolio_return(returns, weights)` backs an optional `weights` argument on `top_n_portfolio_return`, `bottom_n_portfolio_return`, and `model_selected_portfolio_return` (ticker-indexed weights, renormalized over selected names; covered by `tests/test_benchmarks_and_weights.py`). Remaining: source point-in-time caps and thread per-year weight series through the study/sweep call sites.*
- [ ] **Confidence-weighted portfolios (the "most sensible" scheme, currently unsimulated).** Give the hypothetical model a *score*, not just a label decision: sample a synthetic score per stock, calibrated so that thresholding it reproduces the target (recall, precision), then weight positions by calibrated probability (or Kelly fraction). Compare equal / cap / confidence weighting at the same confusion matrix to isolate the value of confidence information. Note this subsumes recall: a confidence-weighted portfolio over all names with score above a threshold *is* the natural output of a real model.

### 3. Risk / variance measurement

- [ ] **Path-level Monte Carlo — replace per-year q05 compounding.** `compute_cagr(custom_stats["q05"])` compounds the 5th-percentile return of *every* year, i.e. assumes the portfolio hits a 1-in-20 bad draw 13 years running (probability ~0.05¹³) — it's not a meaningful "worst case", it's an incoherent one, and it's currently the sweep's headline number. Instead simulate full paths: for each of K simulations, draw one portfolio per year, compound the drawn returns across the whole period, and take quantiles of the resulting *CAGR distribution*. Report `P(path CAGR < benchmark CAGR)` — the probability of underperforming over the full period — as the primary answer to "how often / what's the risk". Per-year q05/q95 stay useful as within-year dispersion diagnostics.
- [ ] **Analytic mean/variance of the selected portfolio.** No Monte Carlo needed for the first two moments: with TP positives and FP negatives drawn uniformly without replacement, the equal-weighted portfolio's expected return is exactly `precision · mean(positive returns) + (1 − precision) · mean(negative returns)`, and its variance is a hypergeometric-sampling expression in the within-class return variances, shrinking with portfolio size — i.e. with recall, via `TP = round(recall·T)`. Together these are the cleanest quantitative statement of the project's thesis: *precision buys expected return, recall buys certainty of getting it.* Implement the closed forms, verify against the simulator, and use them to make the sweep near-instant; keep Monte Carlo for quantiles, since positive-class returns are right-skewed and mean/variance alone understate how often a low-recall draw lands below the mean.
- [x] **"Miss the super-performers" sampling mode (the original PickN intent).** Positive-class returns are heavily right-skewed: a few super-performers carry a large share of the positive-set mean, and a realistic model should not be credited with finding them. Add a mode that excludes the top-k (or top decile) positives from the TP pool before drawing, so low-recall portfolios are built only from ordinary criterion-meeting stocks. The gap between this and uniform draws quantifies how much of the apparent edge depends on catching outliers nobody should count on catching — arguably the single most important pessimism knob for the low-recall thesis. Done: `--exclude-top K_OR_FRAC` on `study` and `sweep` (>= 1 = top-K count, in (0,1) = top fraction of positives, e.g. `0.1` for the top decile), implemented as an `exclude_names` parameter on `simulate_selection`/`estimate_num_ways` — excluded positives stay in recall's denominator (forced false negatives) but can't be drawn, so `TP = round(recall·T)` must be filled from ordinary positives, and ways-counting uses the shrunken pools. Per-year excluded counts surface as `excluded_top` in `custom_stats` and the yearly table, `excluded_top_mean` in the summary and sweep rows. Covered by `tests/test_exclude_top.py` and exclusion cases in `tests/test_simulate_model.py`. The gap is reported in the same run: an identically-reseeded uniform-draw baseline rides along (`custom_stats_uniform`, `*_uniform`/`avg_custom_mean_gap` summary columns, per-year comparison table, dotted plot line; `cagr_custom_q05_uniform`/`custom_q05_gap`/`custom_q05_meets_*_uniform` per sweep cell), so no with/without pair of runs is needed. The FP-side counterpart is the worst-decile stress bound below.
- [ ] **No-pick years: implement the cash policy the study semantics already assume.** Intended behavior when a year has no feasible selection (e.g. `label_threshold=0.1` in a bear year): no picks → hold cash. The current code does something different: `custom_stats` gets NaN and `compute_cagr` drops the year from both the growth product *and the year count* — that is "the year never happened," not cash. Concrete example: returns of +21% then NaN yield CAGR = 21% today, whereas cash in year two yields √1.21 − 1 ≈ 10%; the model CAGR is also then compounded over fewer years than the benchmark CAGR it's compared against. Fix: map no-pick years to a 0% (or T-bill) return, keep them in the year count, and report how many cash years each configuration hit. Same policy in path simulation.
- [ ] **Richer risk metrics on simulated paths**: max drawdown of the growth path, tracking error vs benchmark, Sortino ratio, and worst single-year return — quantiles of CAGR alone hide sequencing risk.
- [ ] **Minimum-portfolio-size guard.** Low recall × few positives can yield portfolios of 1–5 names, where the q05 mostly measures idiosyncratic lottery, not strategy risk, and no real investor would run it. Report portfolio size per year; flag years below a size floor and optionally apply the same cash policy to them.

### 4. Additional concerns (newly identified)

- [ ] **Worst-decile false-positive stress bound.** Uniform-random FP draws stay the base case — *which* negatives a model of a given (recall, precision) confuses is deliberately unmodeled, and sector/factor awareness is assumed to be priced into the stated precision. But for the "never lose money" framing, damage-per-FP matters as much as FP count, so add one pessimistic mode: draw FPs from the worst decile of negatives. This bounds how bad a given precision can get when the mistakes are maximally costly, and pairs with the "miss the super-performers" TP mode above as the two pessimism knobs.
- [ ] **Correlated-luck sensitivity in path simulation.** Independent draws across years is the natural base case, but bounding the other side is cheap: comonotone paths (re-use the same quantile rank of the within-year draw distribution every year) show how wide the CAGR band gets if draw luck persists. Note the current headline number — compounded per-year q05 — is exactly the comonotone rank-0.05 path, i.e. the extreme end of this spectrum; reporting both bands puts it in context.
- [ ] **Calendar-year cohorts are an arbitrary discretization.** Results may be sensitive to the January formation date (turn-of-year effects, one specific 12-month window per year). Sensitivity check: run overlapping 12-month cohorts starting each month (or quarter) and confirm the required-precision conclusions are stable across formation months. Fits naturally with the multi-year-horizon refactor.
- [ ] **Small and regime-biased year sample.** Default 2012–2024 is 13 observations from a mostly relentless bull market; "beats the market" conclusions may not transfer. Mitigations: extend back to 2000 (Sharadar has landed and SEP history reaches ~1998, so the data now supports it — re-run and inspect the coverage report); bootstrap over years (resample years with replacement in path simulation) to widen the CAGR confidence bands; and report results split by regime (benchmark up-years vs down-years) so readers see where the edge comes from.
- [ ] **Feasibility tolerance and small-T rounding artifacts.** `simulate_custom_portfolio_distribution` declares a cell feasible only if achieved metrics land within ±0.1 of the request, and `TP = round(recall·T)` quantizes hard when T is small — so heatmap "n/a" cells can be rounding artifacts rather than truly infeasible requests, and achieved metrics can sit far from grid labels. Surface achieved-vs-requested deviation in the heatmap (annotate cells), make the tolerance a parameter, and consider targeting the closest *achievable* confusion matrix instead of rejecting.

### Out of scope (deliberately)

- **Transaction costs, slippage, and taxes.** The project targets long-horizon, value-style positions with at most annual rebalancing; these frictions are second-order to the precision/recall question being tested. Revisit only if the conclusions turn out to hinge on sub-percent edges.
- **Modeling how a real classifier's precision varies with market regime.** The (recall, precision) pair is treated as a given property of the hypothetical model, not derived from an assumed skill process — the study's question is what trade-offs a model builder should accept, not whether a particular model achieves them. (The per-year base-rate report above is the descriptive stand-in: it shows which years a constant precision is implicitly hardest to sustain in, without taking a position on it.)
